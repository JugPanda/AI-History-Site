Helpful mental models

Three big concepts that make AI history easier to understand

1. AI has repeatedly switched paradigms

The field moved from hand-written logic and search, to expert systems, to probabilistic models, to neural nets, to deep learning and transformers. Each wave solved different problems well—and hit different ceilings.

2. Progress depends on hardware and data, not just ideas

Better algorithms matter, but so do GPUs, memory bandwidth, datasets, and infrastructure. Many ideas became useful only when compute caught up.

3. AI winters happened when promises outran reality

The history of AI is not a straight line upward. Funding and public enthusiasm collapsed more than once when systems failed outside narrow demos.

Why today feels different

Four turning points that changed the trajectory

1956

Dartmouth gave the field a name

The field became legible. Once "artificial intelligence" existed as a shared project, funding, research agendas, and public expectations followed.

1986

Backpropagation made layered learning practical

Neural networks stopped being just an appealing metaphor and became trainable systems with a credible path to useful representation learning.

2012

Deep learning won on modern hardware

AlexNet showed what happens when large neural nets, abundant data, and GPU acceleration align at the same moment.

2017–present

Transformers scaled to general-purpose systems

Large language models pushed AI from narrow-task tools toward flexible systems that can write, classify, reason, summarize, and act across interfaces.

Read the field through two lenses

What a newcomer should notice vs. what a technical reader should notice

For new viewers

  • AI is older than most people think.
  • There was never one single path to “intelligence.”
  • The field repeatedly overpromised and then rebuilt itself.
  • Today’s tools feel magical partly because older failures taught the field what not to do.

For technical readers

  • Representation learning beat brittle feature engineering in many domains.
  • Benchmarks, data scale, and accelerator economics are as important as architectures.
  • Paradigm shifts often came from combining old ideas with newly affordable compute.
  • Modern progress still inherits open problems from earlier eras: grounding, robustness, reasoning, and alignment.
